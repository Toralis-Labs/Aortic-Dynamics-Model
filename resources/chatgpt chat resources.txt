What the IFUs collectively need from a CTA/DICOM workflow

Across these four manufacturers, the core IFU-screening job is to turn a CTA into a structured anatomical report that answers six questions:

Is there adequate access from femoral/iliac arteries for the delivery system?
Is there an adequate proximal seal zone below or at the renals?
Is there an adequate distal seal / fixation zone in the iliac system?
Are the diameters in the usable ranges for the device family?
Are the angles, tortuosity, thrombus, and calcification acceptable for fixation/sealing and delivery?
For branch/fenestrated devices, are the target branch vessels in the right positions and of the right size/patency?

aortic centerline and iliac centerlines,
lowest renal artery location,
proximal neck length,

infrarenal and suprarenal angulation,
distal iliac fixation lengths,
common/external/internal iliac diameters,

thrombus and calcification burden at seal zones,

≥4 mm and <10 mm neck only when used with Heli-FX EndoAnchor and with insignificant calcification / insignificant thrombus, ≤60° infrarenal and ≤45° suprarenal angulation;
≥15 mm neck with insignificant calcification / insignificant thrombus and up to ≤75° infrarenal and ≤60° suprarenal angulation in the broader Europe text.

So the CTA features your software would need for Endurant screening are:

lowest renal artery landmark,
proximal neck start/end,
neck diameter profile along the first 10–15 mm,
whether neck diameter stays within 10%,
infrarenal angulation,
suprarenal angulation if using the more expansive Europe-facing criteria,
distal iliac seal-zone length,
iliac diameters,
femoral/iliac access suitability,
thrombus burden at proximal seal zone,
calcification burden at proximal seal zone,
and general morphology flags such as tortuosity or anatomy that would make delivery incompatible.

For Medtronic’s juxtarenal/parallel-graft language, the required CTA outputs expand further to:

aortic neck diameter 19–30 mm,
available proximal seal length >15 mm,
renal artery take-off angle <90°,
and branch geometry sufficient for one or two renal parallel covered stents. That means the software would have to segment and quantify both renal ostia, centerlines, and renal-to-aortic take-off angles, not just the infrarenal neck.

For the standard GORE EXCLUDER AAA, the publicly visible IFU/spec pages emphasize:

adequate iliac/femoral access,
infrarenal aortic neck treatment diameter 19–32 mm,
minimum aortic neck length 15 mm,
proximal aortic neck angulation ≤60°,
iliac treatment diameter 8–25 mm,
and iliac distal vessel seal zone length at least 10 mm.

For the EXCLUDER Conformable AAA, the IFU-style criteria shift for hostile neck anatomy:

neck treatment diameter 16–32 mm,

and distal iliac seal zone length at least 10 mm. Gore’s positioning pages also explicitly market the Conformable device for short and highly angulated necks, with the company highlighting “short neck down to 10 mm,” “high neck angulation up to 90°,” and “small diameter neck down to 16 mm.”

Compared with Cook’s public IFUs, the Gore pages visible in search are less explicit about thrombus/calcification thresholds. What is clearly public is that Gore’s on-label logic is driven by:

access,

neck length,
neck angulation,
iliac diameter,
distal iliac seal length,

So the CTA outputs your software would need for Gore screening are:

infrarenal neck diameter,
infrarenal neck length,
proximal neck angulation,
iliac distal seal-zone length,

4. Cook Zenith Flex / Zenith Fenestrated / Zenith Iliac Branch: what the IFUs are asking for

Cook’s IFUs are the richest of the four in terms of explicit anatomic detail.

4A. Zenith Flex AAA

For Zenith Flex AAA, the public product pages say it is for abdominal aortic or aorto-iliac aneurysms with morphology suitable for endovascular repair, and the platform is built around:

main body sizing to aortic vessel outer diameter,
iliac leg sizing,
ancillary components,
and introducer-sheath compatibility. The indexed product pages expose detailed sizing tables, but the most explicit anatomic exclusion language available in public search snippets is stronger in the fenestrated and iliac-branch IFUs than on the generic Zenith Flex product page.
4B. Zenith Fenestrated AAA

For Zenith Fenestrated AAA, Cook’s public IFU and product pages explicitly require:

adequate femoral/iliac access,
a non-aneurysmal infrarenal neck of at least 4 mm,
neck diameter 19–31 mm measured outer wall to outer wall,
angle <45° relative to the long axis of the aneurysm,
angle <45° relative to the axis of the suprarenal aorta,
ipsilateral iliac distal fixation site >10 mm in length and 9–21 mm in diameter,
contralateral iliac distal fixation site >10 mm in length and 7–21 mm in diameter.

Cook’s fenestrated IFU is also the clearest one about adverse anatomy at seal zones. It explicitly says key anatomic elements that may affect successful exclusion include:

severe proximal neck angulation,
short proximal aortic neck (<4 mm),
greater than 10% increase in diameter over 15 mm of proximal neck length,
circumferential thrombus and/or calcification at arterial implantation sites,
and irregular calcification and/or plaque that may compromise fixation and sealing. It also warns that such necks may be more conducive to graft migration.

Cook also gives unusually detailed pre-op imaging requirements for fenestrated planning. It recommends high-resolution CT with:

scan from above the diaphragm to the proximal femur,
slice thickness ≤1.0 mm,
at least 50% overlap,
reconstruction algorithm 1 mm throughout,
and contrast 100–200 cc tailored to the patient. It specifically states CT is used to determine graft size (diameter and length) and fenestration/scallop location.

Cook’s fenestrated IFU also tells you exactly which branches matter. During completion angiography and follow-up, it calls for evaluation of:

aorta,
celiac artery,
superior mesenteric artery (SMA),
right and left renals and/or accessory renals,
right and left internal iliac arteries,
right and left external iliac arteries.

So a CTA-to-IFU algorithm for Zenith Fenestrated would have to output:

lowest renal artery and suprarenal aorta axis,
center-to-center ostial locations for celiac, SMA, each renal, and accessory renals if present,
branch diameters,
branch patency,
branch take-off geometry,
proximal neck length,
neck diameter outer wall to outer wall,
neck conicity / diameter expansion over 15 mm,
thrombus and calcification at implantation sites,
iliac fixation lengths and diameters,
and enough geometric information to place fenestrations/scallops relative to branch ostia.
4C. Zenith Iliac Branch

For Zenith Iliac Branch, the IFU gets even more specific about the iliac anatomy. It requires:

common iliac artery diameter at the internal iliac level (luminal diameter) ≥16 mm,
adequate iliac/femoral access for a 20 Fr system,
non-aneurysmal external iliac fixation segment length ≥20 mm with outer-wall diameter 8–11 mm,
non-aneurysmal internal iliac fixation segment length ≥10 mm (preferably 20–30 mm) with outer-wall diameter 7–10 mm.

The iliac-branch clinical study tables in the IFU show the sort of features the device family actually measures pre-procedure:

external iliac artery (EIA) outer diameter and fixation length,
internal iliac artery (IIA) inner and outer diameter and fixation length,
CIA maximum diameter,
EIA minimum access diameter,
CIA length from aortic bifurcation to iliac bifurcation,
IIA length from ostium to anterior-posterior division,
angle of IIA to EIA,
angle of right CIA to left CIA,
circumferential location of IIA from 0–360°.

CIA, EIA, IIA segmentation,
